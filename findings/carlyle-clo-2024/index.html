Validity is formal verification software for high-stakes legal and financial documents. It converts contractual obligations into a logical model and proves whether they can all be satisfied under defined conditions. If they cannot, Validity pinpoints the exact failure state: which obligations conflict, when the conflict arises, and why the document cannot resolve it.

Carlyle Direct Lending CLO 2024-1 is a $431.6 million middle-market collateralised loan obligation managed by Carlyle Global Credit Investment Management. Issued in October 2024 and rated by S&P Global Ratings, the transaction securitises a portfolio of senior secured loans across multiple note classes, ranging from AAA-rated senior notes to BBB-rated junior notes. Its indenture sets the transaction's operating logic: how loan proceeds are collected, how available cash is allocated, and how payments are distributed to noteholders through the Priority of Payments waterfall.

Validity ran the unmodified indenture as filed on SEC EDGAR. No clauses were pre-selected, and no findings were assumed. The pipeline extracted the binding obligations from the waterfall provisions, translated them into formal constraints, and submitted the resulting model to a theorem prover. The test was narrow: whether, under defined stress conditions, all payment obligations in the waterfall could be satisfied at the same time.

Validity identified a conditional stress-state exposure in the Priority of Payments. Under Section 11.1.1.1, clauses (F), (H), (K), and (M) each require payment through the Debt Payment Sequence when a coverage test fails. Each trigger draws on the same finite pool of available Interest Proceeds. The indenture does not impose an aggregate cap for simultaneous coverage test failures. As a result, under a stress state where multiple tests fail at once, the combined Debt Payment Sequence obligation can exceed available proceeds, leaving lower-priority obligations unsatisfied.

This condition is not readily visible through clause-by-clause legal review. Each trigger is clear in isolation. The exposure arises only when the provisions operate together: a system-level property, not a defect in any single clause. This is precisely the class of risk formal verification exists to detect.

Left unidentified, this condition creates potential exposure for junior noteholders, trustees executing waterfall instructions under stress, and funds making allocation decisions based on assumed payment behaviour. In a distressed scenario, the gap between expected and provable payment outcomes can translate directly into mispriced risk, disputed distributions, and litigation risk.

A fund using Validity before committing capital would know, with mathematical certainty, the precise conditions under which its payment entitlement cannot be fully satisfied. It would enter the transaction with a verified account of the structure's stress behaviour, not an assumed reading of the waterfall.
