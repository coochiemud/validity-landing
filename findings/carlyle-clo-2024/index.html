Carlyle Direct Lending CLO 2024-1

A structural payment conflict in a rated, issued CLO indenture — undetected by conventional review, proven in seconds by formal verification.

Indenture dated October 29, 2024  ·  Verified June 2026  ·  SEC EDGAR

01 — The Document

Carlyle Direct Lending CLO 2024-1, LLC

Carlyle Direct Lending CLO 2024-1, LLC is a collateralised loan obligation managed by Carlyle, Inc. and issued under an indenture dated October 29, 2024. The CLO securitises a portfolio of broadly syndicated and middle-market loans. Wilmington Trust, National Association acts as Trustee.

The capital structure comprises U.S.$348.5 million in rated debt across seven note classes. Class A-1 Notes ($92.5M), Class A-L1 Loans ($104M), and Class A-L2 Loans ($50M) are rated AAA(sf) by S&P. Class A-2 Notes ($17M) are also AAA(sf). Class B Notes ($25.5M) are AA(sf). Class C Notes ($34M, deferrable) are A(sf) and Class D Notes ($25.5M, deferrable) are BBB-(sf). Preferred Interests carry a notional amount of $83.1M.

The indenture was filed with the SEC on EDGAR. Validity ran its verification engine against the unmodified filing — no annotations, no modifications, no prior knowledge of the document's structure.

The indenture was reviewed by counsel, rated AAA to BBB- by S&P, and issued to institutional investors. Nothing in the rating process, the legal opinions, or the offering documents identified the structural condition Validity found. It was not visible to qualitative review. It was visible to formal logic.

02 — What Validity Found

A coverage test cascade with no aggregate cap under stress

Every CLO indenture contains a Priority of Payments — a waterfall that determines who gets paid, in what order, and under what conditions. When that waterfall contains multiple independent payment triggers drawing from the same finite pool, with no aggregate cap on simultaneous draws, the document contains a structural condition that conventional review cannot quantify: a provable point at which the payment commitments cannot all be honoured at once.

The Priority of Interest Proceeds (Section 11.1.1.1) contains three conditional draw clauses triggered by coverage test failures. Clause (F) triggers a draw under the Debt Payment Sequence if either Class A/B Coverage Test is not satisfied. Clause (H) triggers a draw if either Class C Coverage Test is not satisfied. Clause (K) triggers a draw if either Class D Coverage Test is not satisfied. Clause (M) triggers a further draw if the Interest Diversion Test is not satisfied during the Reinvestment Period.

Each draw is sized "to the extent necessary" to cause the relevant coverage test to be satisfied on a pro forma basis. No provision specifies an aggregate cap on simultaneous draws across multiple concurrent coverage test failures.

Section 11.1.2 — the insufficiency clause — instructs the Trustee that if available funds are insufficient to make all required disbursements, payments shall be made "in the order and according to the priority set forth under the Priority of Payments… to the extent funds are available therefor." That is the operative limit: funds are applied in waterfall order until exhausted.

Under a stress state in which multiple coverage tests fail simultaneously and available funds fall below the aggregate Debt Payment Sequence requirement, the obligations at clauses (F), (H), (K), and (M) compete for the same finite pool. The combined draw obligation is provably unresolvable from limited available funds. This is not a drafting error. It is a provable structural condition — the kind that formal verification surfaces where qualitative review characterises only an expected outcome.

03 — The Formal Finding

Conditional — not a universal contradiction

Conditional
Stress Exposure Detected
Under a specific, reachable stress-state assignment, the waterfall's coverage test draw obligations cannot all be satisfied simultaneously from available funds. The conflict is formally provable; its activation depends on the stress state.

The significance is not that junior noteholders go short under stress. Every structured finance lawyer knows that. The significance is that the exact conditions under which they go short — and the precise interaction between coverage test triggers that creates that outcome — can now be expressed as a formal proof, not a risk estimate. That proof is deterministic. It does not depend on assumptions. It is either true or it is not.

A conditional finding differs from a universal contradiction. Validity's solver does not conclude that the document is broken. It identifies a truth assignment — a specific combination of fund shortfall and simultaneous coverage test failures — under which the commitments cannot all be satisfied.

The stress-state assignment: available Interest Proceeds are insufficient to cover the combined Debt Payment Sequence draws required to restore all breached coverage tests to pro forma compliance. Under this assignment, the Trustee's obligation to disburse in waterfall order (per Section 11.1.2) cannot simultaneously honour all triggered draw clauses. The solver returns conditional: the conflict exists, is reachable, and is formally proven for the assigned state.

The waterfall resolves the conflict mechanically — senior claims are paid first, junior classes go short. That is how CLO structures are designed to function under stress. The significance is not that the conflict is unresolvable by the document, but that its existence under specifiable conditions is a structural feature that can be proven, not merely estimated.

05 — What This Is Not

Not a defect. A proof that the conflict exists.

This is not a finding that the Carlyle CLO is defective, mis-drafted, or legally problematic. CLO waterfalls are designed precisely to allocate losses to junior classes when funds are insufficient. The priority structure in this indenture functions as intended — under stress, senior claims are paid first and junior classes absorb the shortfall.

The document is legally coherent. Section 11.1.2 provides explicit instruction: disbursements proceed in waterfall order to the extent funds allow. There is no ambiguity about what happens when funds run out.

What Validity surfaces is different: a formal object that proves the conflict exists under a specifiable stress state. A qualitative analyst reviewing the same document might correctly observe that junior classes may not receive full entitlement when multiple coverage tests fail and funds are short. That is true. But it is an observation about an expected outcome, not a proof about a structural condition.

Validity produces the latter. The proof is deterministic, independently reproducible, and expressed as a formal object — not a risk opinion, not a scenario estimate.

06 — Why It Matters

Stress testing, coverage sensitivity, and structural pattern recognition

The pattern Validity found in this indenture is not unique to Carlyle. Any CLO waterfall that conditions Debt Payment Sequence draws on coverage test failures, without an explicit aggregate cap on simultaneous triggers, creates an analogous structural exposure. The specific thresholds, note classes, and fund dynamics will differ. The structural logic is identical.

For a credit analyst: the scenario in which this conflict activates is exactly the scenario your stress models are designed to assess. A formal proof of the conflict structure is a more precise input than a scenario estimate.

For a structured finance lawyer: this is the kind of structural condition that does not appear in a legal opinion because it is not a question of law. It is a question of logic. Validity answers it.
