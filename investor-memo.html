Why This Should Exist

The Problem: Decisions Fail Before They Fail Publicly

Most catastrophic decisions do not collapse because the data was unavailable, the intelligence was missing, or the people involved were unintelligent.

They fail earlier.

They fail at the level of reasoning.

Before money is lost, reputations destroyed, or policies unravel, there is almost always a quieter failure upstream:

  • Unexamined assumptions stack on top of each other
  • Correlation is treated as causation
  • Confidence grows faster than evidence
  • Risks are rhetorically minimised rather than analytically addressed
  • Conclusions feel "obvious" without being logically secured

These failures are rarely malicious. They are structural. And they recur across sectors.

Investment committees approve deals that later implode. Governments adopt policies that produce perverse outcomes. Legal strategies proceed on fragile causal claims. Corporate boards sign off on initiatives that were never logically sound to begin with.

In almost every case, the failure was already present in the document.

The Gap: We Audit Everything Except Reasoning

Modern institutions are saturated with review mechanisms:

  • Financial audits
  • Legal compliance
  • Cybersecurity assessments
  • ESG frameworks
  • Risk registers
  • Fact-checking and verification

Yet there is no standard tool that audits the logic of a document before it becomes a decision.

We check:

  • Whether the numbers add up
  • Whether the claims are legally defensible
  • Whether the language is acceptable

We do not systematically check:

  • Whether the conclusions follow from the premises
  • Whether assumptions are explicit, justified, or stacked
  • Whether causal claims are actually demonstrated
  • Whether uncertainty is being acknowledged or rhetorically concealed

As a result, institutions routinely approve decisions that are logically fragile but procedurally compliant.

Validity exists to address that gap.

What Validity Is

Validity is a reasoning-analysis engine that audits the logical structure of high-stakes documents before they become decisions.

It does not fact-check claims. It does not optimize language. It does not replace human judgment.

It does one thing: it maps the reasoning inside a document and identifies structural failures—assumptions treated as facts, causal leaps without mechanism, risks buried in confident language, evidence selectively deployed.

How it works:

  • A user uploads an investment memo, policy brief, legal argument, or strategy document
  • The system analyzes the logical structure: claims, premises, inference chains, counterfactuals, uncertainty acknowledgment
  • It returns a diagnostic report: reasoning score, failure taxonomy, location of flaws, actionable recommendations

The output is not a judgment of whether the conclusion is "right" or "wrong." It is an assessment of whether the conclusion is justified by the reasoning provided.

What makes this possible now:

Large language models can parse complex documents and identify reasoning patterns at scale. What previously required trained logicians reviewing documents line-by-line can now be systematized.

But the real unlock is not technical—it is institutional. Organizations are beginning to understand that process compliance does not guarantee reasoning quality. The appetite for epistemic infrastructure is growing.

Why Now

Three forces converge to make reasoning infrastructure both possible and necessary:

1. AI makes systematic analysis feasible

Until recently, auditing reasoning quality required expensive human expertise applied document-by-document. Large language models change the economics. What once required a team of analysts can now be delivered in seconds at negligible marginal cost.

2. Institutions are experiencing reasoning failures at scale

The last decade has produced a mounting catalog of decisions that were procedurally sound but epistemically fragile:

  • Investment theses that assumed permanence where there was cyclicality
  • Policy interventions that confused correlation with mechanism
  • Strategic plans built on assumptions that were never tested

The pattern is consistent: smart people, robust processes, catastrophic outcomes. The missing layer is reasoning verification.

3. Accountability is tightening

Boards, regulators, and stakeholders are no longer satisfied with "we followed the process." They want to know: was the logic sound? Were the assumptions explicit? Were the risks actually assessed or just acknowledged?

Due diligence is expanding beyond financial and legal compliance. Epistemic diligence is the next frontier.

Validity is infrastructure for that shift.
